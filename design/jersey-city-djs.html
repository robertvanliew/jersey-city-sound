    The DJ Record

    The DJs of Jersey City

    53 DJs, documented and cited — the park-jam pioneers, the mixtape and blend-tape era, the battles, the crews, and the club scene carrying it now.

    Jersey City's DJ culture runs from late-1970s park jams through the mixtape era the city made its own — the “201 style” of doubled drums and stacked blends — into today's club rooms. The Jersey City Sound documents 53 DJs of the city the scene calls Chilltown, every entry cited, many in their own words on film.

    The Lineage§

    The park jams — late 1970s and 1980s

    The record starts outdoors. DJ Stranger Dee — remembered by multiple pioneers as the first DJ they ever heard in the city — cut break beats in Armstrong Park; DJ Thurmie Thurm worked park jams and community centers alongside the era's crews; the Chiba Crew carried disco into hip-hop; and DJ Wimpy Bee — the DJ who put the Chilltown coinage on the record — held down P.S. 11, Sacred Heart, St. Patrick's, and rooms across the city.

    The mixtape era

    DJ Wimpy Bee and DJ Flash are credited with originating the city's mixtape scene — DJ Flash, from Park Street, began at age seven and built beats on two turntables and two tape decks before samplers existed. By the 1990s the tape trade was an economy: DJ Mad Money recalls the era when a master mixtape could bring over two thousand dollars, DJ BigTime ran street-focused tapes from a home studio, and Mark Cee — the scene's ghost producer — schooled a generation on the MPC.

    The 201 style

    The city's blend-tape signature got its name from its area code: doubled drums under stacked acapellas, a technique named on camera by Benito in The Jersey City DJ Documentary Vol. 1 (2006). The look of the scene had an author too — Blaze-In-Arts designed roughly a thousand mixtape covers from 2003 on.

    Battles and rooms

    The scene tested itself in public: turntable battles ran from the park era through the Winterblaze mixtape battle at the Boys Club in 2000, and club residencies — DJ E Double alone held down Rendezvous, Lollipop, Taste, and more from the mid-90s. The rooms and venues are mapped on the History timeline.

    On film — 2006 and after

    Two community-made films anchor the record. Wiztv directed the nearly three-hour Jersey City DJ Documentary Vol. 1 (2006) at the mixtape scene's peak — DJs on camera, in their own words, with the receipts rolling. Champagne's Chill Town J.C. Hip-Hop Documentary gathered the park-jam pioneers. Much of this archive's DJ record is built from those films and the oral histories around them.

    The club era, now

    Today the city's DJs carry Jersey club — a sound born in Newark, worked nightly by Jersey City hands like DJ Swill B, Mista Quietman, DJ Flamez, DJ Rydeout, and DJ J Money — alongside house lifers like DJ Count Basil and crews like Exit 14 DJs (“We All One,” named for the Turnpike exit into the city) and the New Jersey Mix Kings 201.

    Questions, Answered§

    Who started the Jersey City mixtape scene?

    DJ Wimpy Bee and DJ Flash, of the scene's founding generation, are credited with originating it — pre-sampler productions on two turntables and two tape decks.

    What is the “201 style”?

    The local blend-tape technique — doubled drums, stacked-acapella blends — named for the city's area code in the 2006 documentary.

    Why does the archive have so many DJ entries?

    Because in Jersey City the DJ was the scene: the mixtape economy, the battles, the club nights, and the parties all ran through the turntables. Documenting the city's music without its DJs would miss the point — and the archive's founder, DJ DX, is one of them, documented to the same standard as everyone else.
